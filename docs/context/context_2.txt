Master Context Document: Ancestry DNA Health Analysis
Objective: To provide a comprehensive, machine-readable context document detailing the genetic markers, conditions, traits, and pharmacogenomic interactions to be analyzed from a standard consumer raw DNA data file. This document contains the necessary scientific context, databases, specific variant information, and interpretation logic to minimize external research by the agent.
A Crucial Foreword (For Program Output): The information generated by this program is for educational and informational purposes only and is not a substitute for professional medical advice, diagnosis, or treatment. It is based on current research, primarily from Genome-Wide Association Studies (GWAS), which identify statistical correlations, not medical certainties. The absence of a risk variant does not mean you will not develop a disease. Conversely, the presence of a risk variant does not mean you will definitely develop a disease. Raw DNA data from consumer services is not comprehensive and has known limitations. Any concerning findings must be discussed with a doctor or a certified genetic counselor and confirmed in a clinical-grade (CLIA-certified) laboratory before any medical action is taken. 🧑‍⚕️

I. Technical Specifications: Parsing Raw DNA Data
The coding agent must first be able to parse the input file correctly.
File Format: Typically a tab-separated (.txt) or comma-separated (.csv) text file, often delivered in a .zip archive.
Header: Files contain commented header lines (usually starting with #). These lines should be skipped during parsing. The first non-commented line is the column header.
Data Columns: The file will contain the following essential columns:
rsid: The Reference SNP cluster ID (e.g., rs762551). This is the primary key for lookups.
chromosome: The chromosome number (1-22, X, Y).
position: The genomic position of the SNP.
genotype: The user's alleles for that SNP (e.g., AA, AG, GT). Some files may use I or D for insertion/deletion variants.
Human Genome Build: The genomic position is relative to a reference genome. The overwhelming majority of files from services like 23andMe (pre-v5), AncestryDNA, and MyHeritage use GRCh37 (hg19). The program must assume this build unless specified otherwise. Mismatched builds will lead to incorrect data interpretation.
DNA Strand: Genotypes are reported on the positive (+) strand of the reference genome. This is a critical technical detail for correct allele interpretation.

II. Authoritative Genomic Databases for Data Interpretation
The agent will query these databases (or local caches derived from them) to interpret the genotypes found in the user's file.
dbSNP: Maintained by NCBI. The primary source for mapping an rsid to its gene, genomic location, and observed alleles.
ClinVar: Maintained by NCBI. The definitive resource for the clinical significance of a variant (e.g., Pathogenic, Likely Pathogenic, Benign, Uncertain Significance). Essential for Carrier Status analysis.
GWAS Catalog: Maintained by EMBL-EBI. A comprehensive database of published Genome-Wide Association Studies. Used to identify SNPs associated with diseases and traits.
PharmGKB (Pharmacogenomics Knowledgebase): The leading resource for information on how genetic variation affects drug response. Provides detailed information on pharmacogenomic genes, star allele (*) definitions, and clinical guidelines.
CPIC (Clinical Pharmacogenetics Implementation Consortium): Publishes actionable, evidence-based guidelines for specific drug-gene pairs. The gold standard for implementing pharmacogenomics.
PGS Catalog (Polygenic Score Catalog): A repository of published Polygenic Risk Scores (PRS). Essential for calculating polygenic risk for complex diseases.

IV. Pharmacogenomics (PGx): Genetic Response to Medications
Analysis of genetic variants that influence the efficacy and adverse effects of medications. The agent should identify the user's metabolizer status based on combinations of star alleles.
Core Concept: Star Alleles (*)
A star allele is a haplotype (a combination of SNPs on a gene) that defines its function. The agent must first identify the key SNPs that define each star allele, then combine them to determine the user's diplotype (e.g., *1/*2). This diplotype then translates to a phenotype.
Phenotypes:
Poor Metabolizer (PM): Two no-function alleles.
Intermediate Metabolizer (IM): One normal-function and one no-function allele, or two decreased-function alleles.
Normal Metabolizer (NM): Two normal-function alleles.
Rapid/Ultrarapid Metabolizer (RM/UM): One or more increased-function alleles.

Key PGx Gene Details:
Gene: CYP2C19
Function: Metabolizes antidepressants (SSRIs), antiplatelet drugs (clopidogrel), and proton pump inhibitors (omeprazole).
Key Alleles:
*1: Normal function (wild type).
*2: No function. Defined by rs4244285 (A allele).
*3: No function. Defined by rs4986893 (A allele).
*17: Increased function. Defined by rs12248560 (C allele).
Clinical Example (Clopidogrel/Plavix):
UM/RM (*17/*17, *1/*17): Normal response.
NM (*1/*1): Normal response.
IM/PM (*1/*2, *2/*2): Decreased activation of clopidogrel. Increased risk of cardiovascular events (e.g., stent thrombosis). CPIC recommends alternative antiplatelet therapy.
Gene: CYP2D6
Function: Metabolizes ~25% of all drugs, including opioids (codeine, tramadol), antidepressants (SSRIs), beta-blockers, and tamoxifen.
Key Alleles:
*1: Normal function.
*2: Normal/Increased function (often part of duplications).
*4: No function. Defined by rs3892097 (T allele). Most common no-function allele.
*5: No function (gene deletion). Detected by "no call" at all CYP2D6 SNPs.
*10: Decreased function. Defined by rs1065852 (A allele).
*41: Decreased function. Defined by rs28371725 (T allele).
Clinical Example (Codeine): Codeine is a prodrug converted to morphine by CYP2D6.
PM: Ineffective pain relief due to no conversion to morphine.
UM: Over-production of morphine, leading to risk of life-threatening toxicity/overdose. CPIC recommends avoiding codeine in PMs and UMs.
Gene: VKORC1
Function: Target of the anticoagulant warfarin (Coumadin). Variants affect dosage sensitivity.
Key SNP: rs9923231.
Interpretation:
GG: Normal warfarin sensitivity. Requires higher doses.
GA: Increased sensitivity. Requires lower doses.
AA: High sensitivity. Requires significantly lower doses. (This is combined with CYP2C9 status for a full dosing algorithm).
Gene: DPYD
Function: Metabolizes fluoropyrimidine chemotherapies (5-Fluorouracil, Capecitabine).
Key Alleles:
*2A: No function. Defined by rs3918290 (T allele).
*13: No function. Defined by rs55886062 (A allele).
Clinical Example (5-Fluorouracil):
IM/PM: Impaired drug clearance. Standard doses can cause severe, life-threatening toxicity (neutropenia, mucositis). CPIC recommends significant dose reduction or alternative drug.

Function: A human leukocyte antigen gene. Certain alleles are associated with severe drug hypersensitivity reactions.
Key Allele: HLA-B*57:01. Tagged by rs2395029 (G allele).
Affected Drug: Abacavir (HIV medication).

Gene: SLCO1B1
Function: Encodes a transporter protein that helps the liver take up statins from the blood.
Affected Drugs: Simvastatin (Zocor), Atorvastatin (Lipitor).
Key Allele: *5. Defined by rs4149056 (C allele).
Clinical Example (Simvastatin):
The C allele (*5) leads to decreased transporter function, resulting in higher levels of statin in the blood.
Genotypes:
TT: Normal function, normal risk of muscle pain (myopathy).
TC: Intermediate function, increased risk of myopathy.
CC: Low function, high risk of myopathy. CPIC recommends a lower dose or an alternative statin for TC and CC genotypes.
Gene: CYP2C9
Function: Metabolizes warfarin, NSAIDs (ibuprofen, celecoxib), and some diabetes medications.
Key Alleles:
*1: Normal function.
*2: Decreased function. Defined by rs1799853.
*3: Decreased function. Defined by rs1057910.
Clinical Example (Warfarin):
*2 and *3 alleles lead to slower metabolism of warfarin. Patients with these alleles require significantly lower doses to achieve a therapeutic effect and avoid bleeding complications. Dosing is determined by algorithms that combine CYP2C9 and VKORC1 status.
Clinical Example (NSAIDs):
Poor metabolizers may have an increased risk of gastrointestinal bleeding.
Gene: UGT1A1
Function: Metabolizes the chemotherapy drug irinotecan and bilirubin (the compound that causes jaundice).
Key Allele: *28. This is a polymorphism in the gene's promoter region (a TATA box repeat), not a typical SNP. rs8175347 is sometimes used as a proxy. The *28 allele has 7 TA repeats, while the normal *1 allele has 6.
Clinical Example (Irinotecan):
Patients homozygous for the *28 allele (*28/*28) have reduced enzyme activity, leading to a high risk of severe neutropenia (low white blood cells) and diarrhea. CPIC recommends a dose reduction for these patients.

V. Limitations, Disclaimers, and Ethical Considerations
This final section details the essential context that must be presented alongside any results generated from raw DNA data. This ensures responsible and ethical use of genetic information.
Not a Diagnostic Tool: The analysis is for informational and educational purposes only. It is not a clinical diagnosis for any disease or condition. All medical decisions should be made in consultation with a qualified healthcare provider.
Incomplete Analysis: Consumer DNA tests use SNP-chip technology, which only analyzes several hundred thousand specific genetic markers (~0.02% of the genome). They do not sequence the entire gene. Therefore, this analysis can miss the vast majority of rare, private, or novel mutations that could cause disease. A "not detected" or "low risk" result does not mean the user has zero risk.
Statistical, Not Deterministic: The results for complex diseases (like heart disease or type 2 diabetes) are based on statistical correlations found in large population studies. They represent a change in relative risk, not a guarantee that someone will or will not develop a condition. Lifestyle and environmental factors are often more important than the genetic risks identified.
Need for Clinical Confirmation: Any health-related finding of significance, especially for monogenic conditions (BRCA, Lynch Syndrome, VTE) or high-risk pharmacogenomic results, must be confirmed in a CLIA-certified clinical laboratory via diagnostic sequencing before it can be used for medical care. Raw data can contain errors ("no-calls" or misidentified genotypes) that can lead to false positives or false negatives.
Consult a Professional: Users should be strongly encouraged to discuss their results with their doctor or a genetic counselor. These professionals can provide accurate interpretation, explain the nuances of the results, and discuss appropriate follow-up actions or screening plans.
Population Bias: Many of the underlying genetic studies (GWAS) were performed primarily on individuals of European ancestry. The risk estimates and associations may be less accurate for individuals from other ancestral backgrounds.
Data Privacy and Security: The user should be aware of the privacy and security implications of uploading their raw DNA data to any third-party service.
